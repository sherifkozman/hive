# Meridian Software Inc. — Liquidity & Leverage Ratio Analysis

All figures in USD thousands.

## 2. Liquidity Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | 2024 | 2025 | Rating (2025) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Current ratio | Current assets / Current liabilities | 22,800 / 17,120 = **1.33x** | 31,875 / 20,950 = **1.52x** | Healthy (1.5–3.0) |
| Quick ratio | (Current assets − Inventory) / Current liabilities | 1.33x (no inventory) | 1.52x (no inventory) | Healthy (1.0–2.0) |
| Cash ratio | Cash / Current liabilities | 12,000 / 17,120 = **0.70x** | 18,500 / 20,950 = **0.88x** | Strong (0.5–1.0) |

**Reading:** Liquidity improved meaningfully year-over-year. Current ratio moved from the low end of "Acceptable" (1.0–1.5) in 2024 to just inside "Healthy" (1.5–3.0) in 2025. Working capital nearly doubled (+92%, from 5,680 to 10,925) because current assets grew 40% while current liabilities grew only 22% — genuine liquidity strengthening, not just a base-effect. Cash ratio is "Strong" in both years, meaning the company could cover 70–88% of its short-term obligations from cash alone even before collecting receivables. Because there is no inventory, the quick ratio equals the current ratio and adds no incremental signal here.

**Flag — deferred revenue inflates current liabilities:** Deferred revenue (current) is ~64% of current liabilities in 2024 and ~65% in 2025. This is unearned SaaS subscription cash already collected; settling it requires delivering service, not paying cash out. Standard liquidity ratios therefore understate Meridian's true short-term cash-cushion — a common distortion for subscription businesses and worth noting when benchmarking against non-SaaS peers (per the "watch for items that distort the liquidity picture" guidance).

## 3. Leverage Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 | Rating (2025) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Debt-to-equity | LT debt / Shareholders' equity | n/a | 9,000 / 5,880 = **1.53x** | 8,000 / 12,425 = **0.64x** | Moderate (0.3–0.8) |
| Interest coverage | EBIT / Interest expense | −2,900 / 600 = **−4.83x** | 2,730 / 550 = **4.96x** | 7,509 / 500 = **15.02x** | Very Strong (>10.0) |
| DSCR (2025 only) | Operating cash flow / Total debt service | — | — | 9,800 / 1,500 = **6.53x** | Strong (>2.5) |

Notes on derivation:
- EBIT reconciled from the income statement (revenue − cogs − sales_marketing − rnd − gna) and cross-checked against reported net income after subtracting interest expense and tax for all three years — it ties out exactly (e.g., 2025: 7,509 − 500 − 1,750 = 5,259 = reported net income), confirming the ratio inputs are internally consistent.
- Total debt service for the 2025 DSCR = 2025 interest expense (500) + the observed LT-debt paydown between the two balance-sheet dates (9,000 → 8,000 = 1,000 of principal), since the fixture discloses no separate amortization schedule. Treat this DSCR as an approximation built from the two available balance sheets rather than a contractual schedule.
- Total-liabilities-to-equity (an alternate, broader leverage view) is 4.44x in 2024 and 2.33x in 2025 — far higher than the debt-to-equity figures above because it includes deferred revenue and payables. Per the debt-to-equity formula in this skill (interest-bearing debt / equity), the narrower 1.53x / 0.64x figures are the correct "leverage" read; the liabilities/equity multiple mostly reflects the same deferred-revenue effect flagged in the liquidity section, not credit risk.

**Reading:** Leverage improved sharply. Debt-to-equity fell from "Elevated" (0.8–2.0) at 1.53x in 2024 to "Moderate" (0.3–0.8) at 0.64x in 2025, driven both by a $1,000 debt paydown and by equity more than doubling (5,880 → 12,425) as retained earnings built up from 2025's net income. Interest coverage climbed from adequate (4.96x) to very strong (15.02x) as EBIT grew faster than interest expense fell. DSCR of 6.53x in 2025 is comfortably above the "Strong" threshold (>2.5x), indicating ample operating cash flow relative to combined interest and principal obligations.

## 4. Concerns to Flag

1. **2023 interest coverage was negative (−4.83x)**, i.e., operating losses that didn't even cover interest expense — worse than the "Distressed" (<2.0x) band. This is prior-year history (not part of the requested balance-sheet years) but is directly visible in the fixture's income statement and is relevant context: the credit profile is only two years removed from operating-loss distress, so the 2024–2025 improvement, while real, is a short trend and should be watched for durability rather than assumed structural.
2. **2024 debt-to-equity (1.53x) sat in the "Elevated" band**, and 2024 current ratio (1.33x) was only "Acceptable," not "Healthy." The balance sheet as of 2024 alone would have warranted caution; the improvement to 2025 is significant but rests on a single year of data.
3. **Only two balance-sheet snapshots are available** (2024, 2025). The skill's method calls for reviewing 3–5 year trends before drawing conclusions; a two-point trend is directionally useful but insufficient to rule out volatility or one-off items (e.g., a large customer prepayment inflating 2025 cash/deferred revenue simultaneously).
4. **Deferred revenue is the largest single current-liability line (~65%)**, which mechanically depresses the current/quick ratios relative to a company with the same cash cushion but no subscription deferred-revenue base. This is a modeling nuance rather than a solvency red flag, but ratio comparisons against non-SaaS peers or benchmarks not adjusted for this would be misleading.
5. **No inventory is disclosed**, so the quick ratio provides no information beyond the current ratio for this company — expected for a software business, but worth noting so the quick ratio isn't over-interpreted as an independent liquidity check.
6. **DSCR is an approximation.** It is built by inferring principal repayment from the change in the LT-debt balance across the two balance sheets rather than from a disclosed amortization schedule, and only one year (2025) has the cash-flow data needed to compute it at all.

## 5. Bottom Line

Meridian's liquidity and leverage both improved materially from 2024 to 2025: current ratio 1.33x → 1.52x, cash ratio 0.70x → 0.88x, debt-to-equity 1.53x → 0.64x, interest coverage 4.96x → 15.02x, with a 2025 DSCR of 6.53x. None of the 2025 ratios fall in a concerning band under the skill's benchmarks. The main watch-items are the short (two-year) trend window, the still-recent 2023 operating-loss/negative-coverage history, and the fact that deferred revenue — not distress — is what makes the current/quick ratios and the liabilities-to-equity multiple look tighter than the company's underlying cash position and true debt leverage actually are.
