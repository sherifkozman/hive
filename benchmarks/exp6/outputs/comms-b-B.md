# Internal Communications Package: Travel & Expense Policy Update

---

## 1. All-Hands Announcement (Email from CFO) — Primary Artifact

**To:** All Employees
**From:** [CFO Name], Chief Financial Officer
**Subject:** Changes to our travel and expense policy, effective August 1

Team,

Starting **August 1**, we're changing how we handle travel expenses. Here's what's happening and why.

**What's changing:**

- **Meals become per diem, not itemized receipts.** For trips under 5 days, you'll get a flat daily meal allowance instead of submitting individual receipts: **$75/day domestic, $110/day international**. No more saving every coffee and dinner receipt — you get the allowance regardless of what you actually spend.
- **Alcohol is no longer reimbursable.** Full stop, on any trip.
- **Airfare must be booked through our corporate travel tool.** If you book airfare on a personal card, it will only be reimbursed if you got pre-approval first. Spontaneous personal-card bookings for flights are no longer reimbursed after the fact.
- **Old expense reports are still honored.** If you traveled before August 1, you have 60 days from today to submit those reports under the old rules, as long as the trip itself happened within 30 days of your submission.

**Why we're doing this:**

Finance ran a 2-month pilot with the sales org before rolling this out company-wide. The results: reimbursements processed **40% faster**, and **92% of pilot participants** said they were satisfied with the new process. That's a real, measured improvement, not a hunch — and it's why we're confident enough to extend it to everyone.

**Let's be straight about the trade-off:**

This is genuinely a mix of good and less-good news. Faster reimbursements and less receipt-wrangling is a real win, especially for anyone who's ever lost a receipt or spent an evening reconstructing a week of meals for an expense report. But it does mean less flexibility in a few ways: if you spend less than the per diem, you don't get to pocket the difference and if you spend more on meals on a given day, you're covering the gap yourself. And booking airfare only through the corporate tool removes some of the freedom people have had to shop around or use points on their own — we know that matters to some of you, and we're not pretending otherwise. We think the trade is worth it, but we wanted to name it plainly rather than bury it.

**What you need to do:**

1. For any trip departing on or after August 1, book airfare through [corporate travel tool] — not a personal card, unless you've gotten pre-approval in advance.
2. Stop collecting individual meal receipts for trips under 5 days; you'll be paid the per diem automatically.
3. If you're mid-trip or have a trip booked before August 1, the old rules still apply for that trip — see the transition guidance below.

Managers have talking points to help answer team questions, and we've put together an FAQ covering the most common ones. If you run into a situation the FAQ doesn't cover, reach out to [Expense Policy Team contact] — there are a few edge cases (trips of 5+ days, hotel booking, the pre-approval process itself) that we're still finalizing and will follow up on directly.

Thanks for bearing with us as we tighten this up. This should mean less time on paperwork and faster money back in your pocket.

[CFO Name]

## 3. Manager Talking Points

**Purpose:** Help you field questions from your team after today's announcement. You don't need to become an expense-policy expert — the goal is to reinforce the message, be honest about the trade-offs, and route anything you can't answer.

**Key points to hit:**

- This takes effect **August 1**. Anything booked/traveled before then follows the old rules (with a 60-day window to submit, as long as it's within 30 days of the trip).
- Three changes: meals go to per diem for trips under 5 days ($75/day domestic, $110/day international), alcohol is no longer reimbursable, and airfare has to go through the corporate travel tool unless pre-approved.
- This isn't a cost-cutting move dressed up as a favor — it's genuinely faster for most people (proven in a 2-month sales pilot: 40% faster reimbursement, 92% satisfaction), but it does trade away some flexibility, especially around booking airfare independently. Don't oversell it as a pure win — acknowledge the trade honestly if someone pushes back.
- If someone on your team travels frequently and has strong opinions on this (frequent flyer program preferences, corporate card issues, etc.), that's useful feedback — funnel it to [Expense Policy Team contact] rather than debating the policy yourself.

**Things you can say confidently:**
- "The per diem is a flat rate — you don't need receipts for meals under 5-day trips anymore."
- "No alcohol reimbursement, on any trip, starting Aug 1."
- "Book flights through [corporate travel tool]. If you have a real reason to use a personal card, get pre-approval first — don't assume it'll be reimbursed after the fact."
- "If you traveled before August 1, you're still covered under the old rules — just submit within the normal window."

**Things you should NOT guess at — escalate instead:**
- What happens on trips of 5 days or longer (per diem policy for these hasn't been specified to us yet).
- Whether hotel bookings also have to go through the corporate tool, or just airfare.
- What the actual pre-approval process/timeline looks like for personal-card airfare.
- Whether unused per diem (spent less than the daily amount) can be kept, or extra costs beyond it are ever covered.
- Any one-off exception requests — route these to [Expense Policy Team contact], don't approve informally.

**If someone's frustrated:** Acknowledge it directly — "Yeah, losing the ability to book your own flights is a real change, and I get why that's annoying." Don't argue them out of the feeling. Point them to the pilot data if they want evidence this isn't just a guess, and to the FAQ/policy team for anything specific.

## 4. FAQ

*Question*: What exactly is changing, and when?
*Answer*: Starting August 1: meals on trips under 5 days move to a flat per diem instead of itemized receipts ($75/day domestic, $110/day international), alcohol is no longer reimbursable, and airfare must be booked through the corporate travel tool (personal-card bookings only reimbursed with pre-approval).

*Question*: Why are we moving to per diem instead of itemized meal receipts?
*Answer*: Finance piloted this with the sales org for 2 months and saw reimbursements processed 40% faster, with 92% of participants satisfied with the new process — it cuts the admin work of saving and submitting every receipt.

*Question*: What if my trip is 5 days or longer — do I still get a per diem?
*Answer*: The per diem rates given apply to trips under 5 days. The policy for trips of 5 days or more hasn't been specified — this is something the policy team needs to clarify, and we'll follow up once it's confirmed rather than guess.

*Question*: How is "domestic" vs "international" determined for the per diem rate?
*Answer*: Not specified yet — presumably based on trip destination, but the exact definition (e.g., how layovers or multi-country trips are handled) is something the policy team needs to spell out.

*Question*: Can I still expense alcohol at a client dinner or team event while traveling?
*Answer*: As stated, alcohol is no longer reimbursable — this applies broadly to travel expenses. If there's a distinction for client entertainment specifically, that hasn't been called out, so treat it as not reimbursable until the policy team clarifies otherwise.

*Question*: What happens if my actual meal costs go over the per diem amount?
*Answer*: The per diem is a flat allowance — it doesn't flex up if you spend more, and it isn't stated whether you can keep the difference if you spend less. If you consistently expect costs to exceed it (e.g., expensive-city travel), raise it with the policy team; this hasn't been addressed in what's been shared.

*Question*: Why do I have to book airfare through the corporate travel tool now instead of on my own?
*Answer*: This is a deliberate policy change to standardize bookings; it does mean less flexibility to book independently or use personal rewards/points. If you have a genuine reason to book another way, get pre-approval first — after-the-fact reimbursement for unapproved personal-card bookings won't happen anymore.

*Question*: What if the corporate travel tool doesn't have a flight I need, or it's clearly more expensive than booking directly?
*Answer*: Not addressed in the current policy details — you'd need pre-approval to book elsewhere, but the criteria or process for getting that approval hasn't been defined yet. This is something the policy team needs to publish.

*Question*: Does the corporate-tool requirement also apply to hotels or other travel bookings, or just airfare?
*Answer*: As stated, the mandatory-booking-tool rule applies to airfare specifically. Whether it extends to hotels or other travel costs isn't specified — don't assume it does until the policy team confirms.

*Question*: What happens to trips I already booked or completed before August 1?
*Answer*: Expense reports for travel that happened before the change are still honored under the old (itemized) rules, as long as they're submitted within 30 days of the trip, during a 60-day transition window from the policy's effective date.

*Question*: I have an expense report from a recent trip that I haven't submitted yet — which rules apply?
*Answer*: If the trip itself was within 30 days of when you submit, and you're within the 60-day transition period, it's still processed under the old itemized-receipt rules.

*Question*: Where can I see the pilot data mentioned in the announcement?
*Answer*: The company-wide announcement cites the headline results (40% faster reimbursement, 92% satisfaction from the 2-month sales-org pilot); a fuller breakdown hasn't been shared publicly — ask [Expense Policy Team contact] if you want more detail than what's in the announcement.

*Question*: Who do I contact if my situation doesn't fit any of this (exceptions, edge cases, disputes)?
*Answer*: Route these to [Expense Policy Team contact] rather than assuming an answer — several edge cases (5+ day trips, hotel-booking scope, the pre-approval process itself) are still being finalized by the policy team.
